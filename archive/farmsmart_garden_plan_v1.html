Pot Strategy

Where every pot goes — and why.

Pots are the flexible layer of the plan. They stay movable all season so you can chase the sun, escape a frost, or pull a plant away from a pest outbreak. Three roles for three plants.

Bottle Gourd · 3 pots

Fence-climbing champions

Pot size: 20 gal (50L+) Depth: ≥ 16"
  • BG1 — SW corner: Tucked against the SW fence. Vine climbs straight up and spreads along the SW fence into the back of the garden.
  • BG2 — SE middle: Centered on the SE (lawn-side) fence. Vine runs up and spreads laterally along the SE fence in both directions — this is your longest fence run.
  • BG3 — NE corner: Against the NE fence. Vine climbs up and wraps around the NE corner.
  • Why pots, not beds: Bottle gourd is aggressive (20+ ft vines). Keeping it in pots contains the roots and leaves bed space for other crops.
  • Support: Run jute twine or netting up each fence section. Fruits hang — support heavy ones with cloth slings.
Capsicum · 4 pots

Wall-warmed peppers

Pot size: 5 gal Depth: ≥ 12"
  • CP1 & CP2 — top strip: Along the house wall (NW). The wall banks heat and reflects warmth — capsicum fruits set better at 70–85°F.
  • CP3 & CP4 — bottom strip: Along the SE (lawn-facing) side for raw sunlight throughout the day.
  • Spacing: Keep pots 2 ft apart minimum for airflow — prevents fungal issues we saw with the melons.
  • Stake each plant: Branches snap under fruit load. 3-ft bamboo stake + soft tie per plant.
Green Chili · 6–9 pots

Scattered heat-lovers

Pot size: 3 gal Depth: ≥ 10"
  • GC1–GC5 — top strip: Five pots distributed along the house wall side, filling gaps between capsicum pots.
  • GC6–GC9 — bottom strip: Four more along the SE fence side, giving a continuous chili ribbon across both strips.
  • Why so many: Chilies are prolific — even a few plants feed a household. Spread across pots = spread across risk (one bad pot ≠ no chilies).
  • Pair with basil: A basil plant tucked into each chili pot deters aphids and whiteflies.

Lessons Learned

Why the melons died — and how bottle gourd survives.

Last year's watermelon and muskmelon were wiped out by disease and bugs. Bottle gourd is in the same family (Cucurbitaceae) and will face the same threats. Here's the playbook so history doesn't repeat.

The usual suspects (cucurbit family)

  • Powdery mildew — white powder on leaves, spreads fast in humid weather with poor airflow.
  • Downy mildew — yellow blotches on leaves, thrives in wet foliage.
  • Cucumber mosaic virus — spread by aphids; mottled, stunted growth; no cure once infected.
  • Fusarium / bacterial wilt — vines suddenly collapse from base. Often soil-borne.
  • Cucumber beetles — chew leaves AND spread bacterial wilt. The double threat.
  • Squash vine borers — larvae tunnel inside stems, plant dies from the base up.
  • Aphids & spider mites — sap-suckers; weaken plants and spread viruses.

Prevention playbook (do all of these)

  1. Don't plant cucurbits where melons grew. The SW corner where BG1 sits should be fresh soil — top-dress with compost + neem cake, and avoid reusing last year's soil for BG.
  2. Drip, never spray. Overhead water on leaves = mildew factory. Water at soil level only.
  3. Airflow is medicine. 2-ft spacing between pots, training vines up the fence instead of letting them sprawl on the ground.
  4. Mulch the pot surface. Stops soil splash (which carries fungal spores to lower leaves) and keeps moisture even.
  5. Neem oil, weekly, preventatively. Mix 2 tsp neem + 1 tsp soap per liter. Spray leaves (top & bottom) in evening starting at flowering.
  6. Row cover until flowering. Lightweight fabric over young bottle gourd keeps beetles and moths out. Remove when flowers appear so pollinators can visit.
  7. Companion allies. Nasturtiums near pots (trap aphids), marigolds in bed (nematode deterrent), basil with chilies.
  8. Inspect every Sunday. Flip leaves, check stems. Remove anything mottled or collapsing immediately and don't compost it.
  9. Healthy soil = resistant plants. Compost + mycorrhizal inoculant + vermicompost monthly. Strong root biology outcompetes pathogens.

Next up: the smart layer.

Every pot and every bed gets a drip emitter on a zoned 12V solenoid system driven by a Raspberry Pi. Capacitive soil moisture sensors across both beds and in two reference pots will trigger watering based on a per-zone threshold — and skip entirely if rain is forecast. A Pi Camera on a mast will time-lapse the garden and flag leaf diseases early using a TensorFlow Lite model. Home Assistant dashboard on your phone.
